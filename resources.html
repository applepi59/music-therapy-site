Scientific Evidence Supporting Music Therapy

1. Music Therapy as an Intervention for Student Stress (Frontiers in Psychology, 2024)

A randomized study involving 112 undergraduate students implemented a 4-week music therapy intervention consisting of 30-minute guided listening sessions twice a week.

  • Results:
    • Anxiety levels (measured via GAD-7) decreased by 28%
    • Perceived stress levels (PSS scale) dropped by 31%
    • Participants reported improved sleep quality and higher daily motivation
  • These effects persisted two weeks after the intervention, suggesting that music therapy has lasting psychological benefits.

2. Music Genres and Stress Response (Jingning Lin, 2023)

This exploratory study surveyed 384 students across three universities on their emotional responses to various music genres.

  • Findings:
    • 74% of participants identified lo-fi beats and classical music as the most relaxing
    • Students preferred slower tempos, mellow tones, and low-frequency instruments for stress relief
  • Genre-specific music has potential as personalized therapy based on emotional triggers.

3. Music Therapy in Kazakhstan (KazNU Bulletin, 2017)

Conducted at KazNU with students from Atyrau and Almaty, this research focused on the underuse of music therapy in Kazakhstan’s educational institutions.

  • Outcomes:
    • 63% of students noted improved focus and emotional relief after regular sessions with guided music.
    • The authors conclude that music therapy could be a cost-effective and scalable strategy for stress management in local schools and universities.
  • Most participants reported decreased emotional burnout and better academic motivation, especially during exam seasons.

4. Music and Emotional Processing (Koelsch, 2013)

Neuroscientific findings show that music activates brain areas responsible for emotional regulation, including the limbic system, amygdala, and prefrontal cortex.

  • Results:
    • Music listening led to a reduction in cortisol levels (by approx. 25% after a 20-minute session).
    • Participants also experienced increased activity in the insula and anterior cingulate cortex, which are essential for processing emotional responses.
    • Music’s structure allows the brain to mirror safe, predictable patterns, which helps students self-soothe during stress.

5. Polyvagal Theory & Gentle Music Intervention (Porges, 2020)

Based on Polyvagal Theory, music can activate the ventral vagal complex, helping the body shift into a state of calm and social engagement.

  • Results:
    • Soft, rhythmic, and slow-tempo music stimulates the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing heart rate and tension.
    • Students with high anxiety were more responsive to vocally rich, gentle melodies, which mimic the comforting tones of human voice.
    • The theory supports non-verbal, sound-based interventions for mental health in educational environments.

6. Stress Management via Music (ScienceDirect, 2023)

A meta-analysis of 18 clinical trials on music therapy and stress found:

  • Results:
    • An average reduction of 35% in self-reported stress scores
    • Enhanced emotional awareness and coping skills in adolescents and college students
    • Music therapy was especially effective when used before exams, during study breaks, or as part of daily relaxation routines
